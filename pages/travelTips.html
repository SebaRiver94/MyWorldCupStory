Travel Tips

Over the years I've followed Argentina across two unforgettable FIFA World Cups — Russia 2018 and Qatar 2022. The logistics were totally different, the routes were wild, and the lessons were priceless. Here's exactly how I organized each trip, what worked, and what I'd repeat.

Russia 2018

I booked my flight, match tickets, and accommodation after the tournament had already started. Right after the Iceland game, I found a cheap fare with a layover in the United Arab Emirates and jumped in. I stayed about an hour to an hour and a half from central Moscow. The key was being close to railway stations — I could reach the center in roughly 40 minutes, which kept costs down without losing time. For Saint Petersburg I took a direct flight. To reach Kazan, I flew from Saint Petersburg to Ekaterinburg and then took a 12+ hour train because direct flights were very expensive. The train dropped me close to Kazan, which made the switch worth it. After the matches, I flew back to Moscow to catch my return to Argentina.

Qatar 2022

For Qatar 2022 I started a full year in advance because tickets, accommodation, and logistics were all centralized through FIFA. My first booking was a long São Paulo → Abu Dhabi flight. Over the next months I added the missing legs: Buenos Aires → São Paulo and Abu Dhabi → Doha. I stayed in a fan village in Al Wakrah (the supporters' area at the time). Getting to the Corniche took up to two hours, but bus and metro rides were free for fans, which kept everything simple and affordable.

Flights

  • Book the anchor leg first (the hardest/longest flight), then connect the shorter segments later as fares open up.
  • Compare total travel time vs. price — sometimes a long layover beats a pricey direct flight.
  • Watch prices after key matchdays; demand can spike or drop overnight.

Accommodation

  • If you stay outside the center, prioritize walking distance to rail or metro — it saves time and money every day.
  • For tournament housing (fan villages), check transport frequency and first/last train times before you book.
  • Keep one flexible night for unexpected schedule changes or extra celebrations.

Transport

  • In Russia, trains between cities were reliable and could be cheaper than last-minute flights.
  • In Qatar, metro + buses covered most routes for free during the World Cup — plan around stations.
  • Always download offline maps and keep your key routes starred (airport ↔ lodging, stadiums, fan zones).

In both Russia 2018 and Qatar 2022 the Fan ID was mandatory for entry. It's tied to your match ticket confirmation and acts as your supporter permit. Without it, you can be denied boarding or entry even if you hold valid tickets.

I applied through the official website using my ticket confirmation number. I uploaded my documents, completed the form, and linked everything online. Once approved, the Fan ID enabled entry and simplified checks inside the country.

I applied via the official mobile app, again using the ticket confirmation number. The process mirrored 2018 but was fully app-based, with digital verification. Keep the app updated and carry your passport — both are checked frequently.

Final Thoughts

If you're on a tight budget, build around transport and flexibility: trains in Russia saved me when flights spiked, and planning a year ahead in Qatar made the FIFA system work in my favor. Stay close to transit, keep one plan B, and let the football lead the way.
